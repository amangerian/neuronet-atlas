How the four models differ

independent of any condition

The four models in this atlas are not four ways of saying the same thing. Each takes different input, operates on a different timescale, and answers a question the others structurally cannot. This section explains them on their own terms — what each measures, how it is actually computed, and what it can and cannot license you to claim.

In depth

How they fit together

Think of brain activity as a traveller moving through terrain.

The functional network is the map of roads — which places are connected, on average. The dynamic network is the traveller's itinerary — where they actually spent the day and how often they moved. The energy landscape is the terrain itself — the valleys that explain why the traveller kept ending up in the same place. And control theory asks where to build a road, and how much fuel it takes to climb out.

This matters practically: two conditions can have nearly identical average maps but completely different itineraries, and the same itinerary can arise from differently-shaped terrain. Each layer constrains the next — which is why the atlas presents all four rather than picking one.

One caveat worth stating plainly: these models are not independent evidence. Three of the four are computed from the same resting-state BOLD data, so when they agree, that agreement is partly structural rather than four separate confirmations. Control theory is the partial exception — it is usually built on the structural (diffusion) connectome, so it brings genuinely different information.

TRANSDIAGNOSTIC

Comparing all six conditions

shared vs. disorder-specific

Because all six conditions were built on an identical template, they can be compared directly. This view asks the question the atlas is designed to answer: what is shared across psychiatric conditions, and what is specific to each? Anchored where possible to meta-analyses, including Brandl 2018, which explicitly separated schizophrenia-specific dysconnectivity from transdiagnostic effects.
