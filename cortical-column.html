The canonical circuit in one sentence

Driver input from the thalamus arrives in layer 4, is passed up to layer 2/3 for local processing and onward transmission to other cortical areas, then down to layer 5, which is the cortex's main output to subcortical structures, and to layer 6, which projects back to the thalamus and regulates its own input. Meanwhile layer 1 — almost cell-free — carries top-down feedback onto the apical dendrites of pyramidal cells sitting in the layers below, letting context modulate what the column does with its feedforward input.

Two directional streams are worth separating. Feedforward projections originate in layer 2/3 and terminate in layer 4 of the next area up the hierarchy. Feedback projections originate in layers 5/6 and terminate in layer 1, avoiding layer 4 entirely. This asymmetry is the anatomical basis for hierarchical and predictive-processing accounts of cortex.

Why this matters for the network models

The whole-brain networks in the main atlas are built from signals averaged over millions of these columns. The layer architecture explains several things those coarse models can only describe. Layer 5 thick-tufted cells are coincidence detectors: they fire strongly when basal (feedforward) and apical (feedback, via layer 1) input arrive together — a cellular implementation of "context meets evidence." Layer 6 corticothalamic feedback sets the gain on the thalamus, which is directly relevant to the thalamo-cortical dysconnectivity that defines schizophrenia in the network atlas.

Note also that the column is not uniform across cortex. Layer 4 is thick in primary sensory areas, thin in association cortex, and effectively absent in motor cortex (which is why motor cortex is called agranular). The canonical circuit is a strong idealisation, not a literal blueprint for every region.
