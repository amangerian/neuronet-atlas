Pyramidal Neuron & GABAergic Interneurons

About 80% of cortical neurons are excitatory pyramidal cells; the remaining ~20% are GABAergic interneurons. What makes the inhibitory system powerful is that different interneuron classes target different compartments of the same pyramidal cell — and each compartment does a different computational job. Why compartment targeting is the whole point

A pyramidal neuron is not a single summing point. Its basal dendrites receive feedforward drive, its apical tuft in layer 1 receives top-down context, its soma integrates toward threshold, and its axon initial segment is where the spike is actually generated. Because each interneuron class targets one of these compartments selectively, inhibition is not a single volume knob — it is a set of independent controls over distinct stages of the computation.

So PV basket cells, sitting on the soma, act like a gain and timing control on the output: they set how much excitation is needed to fire and enforce the rhythmic windows that produce gamma oscillations. Chandelier cells, uniquely targeting the axon initial segment, sit at the very last checkpoint before a spike. SST/Martinotti cells, reaching up into layer 1, veto the top-down contextual input before it ever reaches the soma. And VIP cells mostly do not touch pyramidal cells at all — they inhibit the SST cells, and so release the apical tuft. That disinhibitory motif is how arousal, locomotion and attention open the cortex to top-down influence.

The classes side by side

PV, SST and VIP cells are largely non-overlapping and together account for the great majority of cortical GABAergic neurons; the remaining group is dominated by layer-1 NDNF/neurogliaform cells. This three-plus-one scheme is the standard modern classification.

The disinhibition cascade

The dominant circuit-level account of schizophrenia begins not in the pyramidal cell but in the PV interneuron. NMDA receptors on fast-spiking PV cells appear to be hypofunctional — the observation that motivated the whole hypothesis is that NMDA-receptor antagonists such as ketamine and PCP reproduce positive, negative and cognitive symptoms in healthy people, which no dopaminergic drug does.

Because PV cells are themselves driven by NMDA receptors, hypofunction there means less excitatory drive onto the inhibitory cell, so the PV cell fires less, releases less GABA, and the pyramidal cells it normally restrains become disinhibited. The downstream consequence is excess and poorly-timed glutamate release, degraded gamma synchrony (PV cells are the gamma generators), and a cortex whose signal-to-noise is compromised. That is the cellular story that sits underneath the "destabilised, poorly-segregated" networks schizophrenia shows at whole-brain scale in the main atlas.

The GABAergic side shows a coherent set of post-mortem changes: GAD67 reduced (less GABA synthesised), parvalbumin reduced, GAT-1 reduced in chandelier cartridges, and — revealingly — GABAA α2 receptors increased on the pyramidal axon initial segment. That last one runs in the opposite direction to the others and is generally read as a compensatory upregulation: the postsynaptic cell adding receptors because less GABA is arriving.

A caution on causality. These are convergent findings, not a demonstrated sequence. Post-mortem tissue reflects a lifetime of illness and antipsychotic exposure; the NMDA-hypofunction model is inferred largely from pharmacological challenge in healthy volunteers; and the dopamine and glutamate accounts have never been fully unified. What is genuinely well established is the pattern: reduced PV-interneuron function and elevated presynaptic striatal dopamine capacity are among the most replicated biological findings in the disorder.

Connecting down from the network models

The excitation–inhibition balance implemented by these cells is the microscale substrate of several macroscale claims in the main atlas. The attractor account of OCD rests on deepened basins produced by excess recurrent excitation — Rolls and colleagues modelled this explicitly as increased NMDA/AMPA conductance, with increased GABAergic drive partially reversing it. In schizophrenia, reduced PV-interneuron function is one of the most replicated cellular findings, and PV cells are precisely the cells that generate gamma rhythms and control pyramidal output gain — a plausible cellular route to the destabilised, poorly-segregated networks seen at whole-brain scale.

The direction of inference matters though: these are convergent stories across scales, not a demonstrated causal chain from interneuron to fMRI network.
